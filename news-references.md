Each entry lists the outlet, publication date, a direct article URL, and a summary.

## How the ball sensor and its graph work

The adidas **Trionda** match ball contains a **500 Hz inertial motion sensor** ("chip in the ball"). Every touch produces a spike on a **Snickometer-style trace ("Snicko")** — a graph that pinpoints the exact instant the ball is struck. That kick-moment feeds **semi-automated offside**, and the same trace can indicate whether the ball made contact with an object. Two decisions put this technology on front pages:

- **Croatia vs Portugal (group stage, ~Jul 2):** the sensor fixed the precise kick instant, and semi-automated offside then ruled Croatia's stoppage-time equaliser out. FIFA publicly defended the "Snicko" call.
- **England vs Norway (quarter-final, ~Jul 11):** the trace was used to check whether the ball hit a **spidercam cable** before Bellingham's equaliser; the sensor showed **no contact spike**, so FIFA let the goal stand. Norway disputed it.

> Background: "Snicko" first appeared at Euro 2024 (Belgium vs Slovakia) — that was 2024, not part of this 2026 set.
